# This function summarizes the mins and maxes of numeric variables
summarize_column <- function(column) {
  if (is.numeric(column)) {
    return(data.frame(
      Type = "Numeric",
      Min = min(column, na.rm = TRUE),
      Max = max(column, na.rm = TRUE)
    ))
  }
}

Results

This analysis was conducted in two parts: the first to assess the relationship between use of the most common AD medications and the likelihood of receiving an AD diagnosis. The second to compare if there were differences in the odds of receiving an AD diagnosis between these medications.

Likelihood of Receiving an AD diagnosis

Primary Explanatory Variable

Patients with 100% PDC adherence to donepezil had 2.94 times higher odds of an Alzheimer’s disease (AD) diagnosis compared to non-adherent individuals (p < 0.0001, 95% CI: 2.12, 4.14).

For galantamine, full adherence was associated with 3.61 times the odds of an AD diagnosis (p < 0.0001, 95% CI: 3.29, 3.97).

Patients who adhered fully to rivastigmine had the highest odds, at 4.60 times greater likelihood of an AD diagnosis (p < 0.0001, 95% CI: 2.66, 8.33).

Similarly, those with 100% PDC for memantine exhibited 2.92 times the odds of an AD diagnosis (p < 0.0001, 95% CI: 2.50, 3.42).

Covariates

Pill burden was a highly significant predictor of an Alzheimer’s disease (AD) diagnosis (p < 0.0001). Each 1-unit increase in pill burden was associated with a 2% lower odds of having an AD diagnosis (OR = 0.98).

Depression also showed a strong association with AD diagnosis (p < 0.0001), with individuals diagnosed with depression having 1.37 times higher odds of an AD diagnosis (z = 6.87).

Similarly, the Charlson Comorbidity Score was a significant predictor (p < 0.0001), where each additional point increase in comorbidity score was associated with a 2% higher odds of an AD diagnosis (OR = 1.02, z = 3.76).

Pairwise Comparisons of Medications

The final model selected by BIC included drug (forced in), pill burden, and age as predictors.

There was no statistically significant difference among drug categories, with all unadjusted p-values exceeding 0.05, indicating that medication type alone did not strongly influence Alzheimer’s disease (AD) diagnosis odds.

However, pill burden was a significant predictor (p < 0.0001), where each additional unit in pill burden was associated with a 1% decrease in the odds of receiving an AD diagnosis (OR = 0.99, 95% CI: 0.98–0.99).

Similarly, age was significantly associated with AD diagnosis (p < 0.0001), with each additional year linked to a 3% decrease in the odds of diagnosis (OR = 0.97, 95% CI: 0.96–0.98).

Discussion

Main Findings

The observed increase in odds of receiving an AD diagnosis among patients with 100% PDC adherence to any medication was unexpected but, upon further reflection, not surprising. This trend likely reflects the fact that patients are more commonly prescribed these medications following an AD diagnosis, rather than the medications themselves influencing diagnosis rates. Additionally, those taking AD medications may receive stronger encouragement from healthcare providers and greater support from caregivers, leading to higher adherence to their treatment regimen.

To better account for this longitudinal progression, an alternative approach—such as a time-to-event analysis until first AD diagnosis—may be more appropriate. This would allow for an examination of the timing of MCI-to-AD progression, rather than simply assessing diagnosis odds retrospectively. A comparative effectiveness analysis could further investigate whether any specific drug delays the transition from MCI to AD more effectively than others.

The lack of a statistically significant difference in AD diagnosis odds between medication groups is noteworthy. This suggests that these drugs may be similar in their effectiveness, implying that the choice between donepezil, galantamine, rivastigmine, and memantine should be based more on patient tolerability and side effect profiles rather than their potential impact on disease progression. This insight could be valuable for providers selecting treatments for MCI patients, as well as for patients and families making informed decisions about therapy options.

Covariates

It was found that pill burden was negatively associated with the odds of receiving an AD diagnosis. This could be for several reasons: Physicians may be more cautious in diagnosing AD in individuals already burdened with extensive medical management, especially if cognitive symptoms are attributed to medication side effects, comorbid conditions, or general aging-related decline rather than neurodegenerative pathology. Additionally, patients prescribed AD-specific medications may have received early cognitive evaluations, which increases their likelihood of being formally diagnosed. Finally, some studies suggest that certain non-AD medications, including antihypertensives and statins, might offer protective benefits against cognitive decline. If high pill burden is associated with regular adherence to such medications, it is possible that some patients experience a slower progression toward AD, thereby reducing their likelihood of diagnosis during the study period.

The strong association between depression and Alzheimer’s disease (AD) diagnosis aligns with existing research, as depressive symptoms are commonly observed in individuals with AD. However, the relationship is complex—depression can be both a risk factor and a symptom of AD. Depression and AD are both linked to neurotransmitter imbalances, particularly involving serotonin and dopamine. Chronic depression may contribute to inflammation and neurodegeneration, potentially increasing susceptibility to AD. Individuals experiencing cognitive changes may develop reactive depression, which complicates distinguishing the primary condition.

It was also found that the Charlson Comorbidity Score was a significant predictor of the odds of receiving an AD diagnosis. This could be because patients with multiple chronic conditions may experience greater physiological stress, inflammation, and vascular issues, which have been linked to accelerated cognitive decline. Similary, cardiovascular diseases, diabetes, and other metabolic disorders captured in the Charlson Index may contribute to cognitive impairment, increasing the likelihood of an AD diagnosis. Other possible explanations involve confounding, such as individuals with higher Charlson Comorbidity Scores tending to have frequent interactions with healthcare providers, raising their chances of undergoing cognitive assessments, thus having higher clinical surveillance for neurodegenerative diseases.

Interestingly, there was a difference in the odds of an AD diagnosis and the patient region. Specifically, patients in the West had about 0.80 times the odds of receiving an AD diagnosis compared to patients in the Midwest and South. This could be due to the West having fewer specialists per capita, potentially lowering the diagnosis rates. Similarly, the Midwest and South may have a higher concentration of academic medical centers and neurologists, leading for greater oppotunities for diagnosis. This could also be explainable by regional differences in age, attitudes towards cognitive health, differences in medical practices, or socioeconomic status, or lower pollution, lower exposure to pesticides, or higher water quality in the West, o. Future studies should follow-up on this thread to identify potential populations that are at-risk for not receiving a proper diagnosis.
